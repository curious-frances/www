Research

A Multi-Segment, Soft Growing Robot with Selective Steering

Alexander M. Kübler, Sebastián Urdaneta Rivera, Frances B. Raphael, Julian Förster, Roland Siegwart, and Allison M. Okamura
Highlighted at IEEE Robot Soft 2023

Everting, soft growing vine robots benefit from reduced friction with their environment, which allows them to navigate challenging terrain. Vine robots can use air pouches attached to their sides for lateral steering. However, when all pouches are serially connected, the whole robot can only perform one constant curvature in free space. It must contact the environment to navigate through obstacles along paths with multiple turns. This work presents a multi-segment vine robot that can navigate complex paths without interacting with its environment. This is achieved by a new steering method that selectively actuates each single pouch at the tip, providing high degrees of freedom with few control inputs. A small magnetic valve connects each pouch to a pressure supply line.

The Smart Car Assistant

Final Project for CS107E Computer Systems From Ground-Up

This project, the final assignment for Stanford University's introductory systems class, involved building Katriana, a smart car designed entirely from scratch. Katriana is equipped with a personal assistant capable of communicating through audio files, with every component—software PWM, audio generation, and streaming—developed from bare metal. The car can move forward, backward, and stop, with multi-colored LEDs providing visual feedback when the speaker is active and the motor drivers are functioning correctly.

First-Person View VR-Enhanced RC Car for Reflex Exploration

Final Project for EE267 Virtual Reality

This project explores the integration of head movements with traditional hand controls in immersive virtual reality (VR) teleoperation, focusing on remote-controlled car navigation. The system combines VR headsets with two control methods: one using keyboard arrow keys and lateral head movements, and another utilizing a mouse-keypad joystick alongside head movements. By analyzing user performance and behavior in these two control scenarios, the project aims to evaluate how head tilting can enhance control intuitiveness and immersion in VR environments. The research contributes to advancing VR teleoperation systems, offering insights into more natural and efficient interaction methods in virtual environments.

Improving Localization and Pathfinding in Robotics Using Monte Carlo

Final Project for CS109 Probability for Computer Scientist

This project integrates two fundamental robotics concepts—localization and pathfinding—into a unified system. Localization enables a robot to estimate its position within an environment, even when faced with noisy sensor data, using Monte Carlo localization and particle filtering. Pathfinding, on the other hand, leverages the A* algorithm to calculate the optimal path to a goal while avoiding obstacles. The system is visualized in real-time through Pygame, allowing for dynamic interaction with the environment. By combining these techniques, the project demonstrates how autonomous systems can effectively navigate and localize within complex and uncertain environments
